About

I completed my bachelors in physics at UC Santa Cruz in 2016, and I recieved my PhD in astronomy from the University of Alabama in 2025. In between I also worked at a start-up company that teaches computer programming to k-12 students using a project-based curriculum. At the University of Alabama I taught introductory physics and astronomy classes. I have also had adjunct positions at West Virginia University, Parkersburg and Union College, New Jersey.

I use simulations, mock observations, and advanced computational methods such as machine learning to find hidden patterns in large datasets. Most of my work uses IlustrisTNG data, but I also use Hubble data to study the photometry and abundances of red giant stars.

Aside from astronomy I am interested in the philosophy of science and epistemology, or, "how we know what we know". As someone who uses machine learning for research, I believe it is important to understand the psychology behind misinformation, and how it spreads.

I have over a decade of experience teaching STEM subjects in a variety of instructional modes and teaching environments. My teaching style is driven by cutting-edge research into effective pedagogy. Recently, I have been interested in active learning and how to introduce it into large lectures. My goal when teaching is always to understand the student first.

I am also interested in science outreach. I helped start the Physics and Astronomy Graduate Student Association (PAGSA) at UA, and created and ran the website for several years. I have given many public talks, and I volunteer at the Tuscaloosa Public Library Repair Cafe, where I teach children and adults about the inner workings of common household electronics.

Research

I use cosmological magnetohydrodynamic simulations to study the gas dynamics of galaxies and how that effects their evolution. "Cosmological" means that a large chunk of the universe is simulated. "Magnetohydrodynamic" means the simulations include electromagnetic forces ("magneto") as well as fluid/thermodynamic forces such as pressure. These simulations contain a huge amount of information, and new techniques are needed to properly process it. Hydrodynamic simulations and machine learning may sound intimidating, but my research is relatively intuitive, and does not require previous experience. I have had a lot of undergraduate assistance in my work, and I'm always happy to have more!

Mass is an assumed driver of differences between galaxies. In my first paper, we stacked IllustrisTNG halos by mass and feedback type to look for correlations in the motion of the gas. By seperating our sample into groups by both star-formation rate and black hole activity, we can isolate the effects of each feedback type. We found that feedback type had a stronger impact on gas dynamics than mass. The image above shows plots of the average mass flow for low feedback halos (top) and star-forming halos (bottom). Inflows (outflows) are negative (positive). Low feedback galaxies have the largest inflow (blue and green lines) near \(0.2R_v\), which serves as a dividing line between the inner and outer CGM. Star-forming halos, however, have the highest inflow at maximum radius, because the larger outflows (red line) from central star-formation reduces the inflow at low radius.

This plot also includes the effects of the "fuzz"-- a collection of particles not easily accessed in the simulation, that nonetheless have significant effects on both gas dynamics and observables such as column density. It is important to compare simulations with observations, but these comparisons will not be even if all mass along a line of sight is measured in observations, and only "member particle density" is measured in simulations. Fuzz particles are more enriched than surrounding halo gas, and tend to move outward, implying they may be connected to feedback events. I had several undergraduates help me with tracking the fuzz between snapshots and characterize their motion.

I also designed the software infrastucture for the Circumgalactic Dictionary. The circumgalactic medium (CGD) is a diffuse but expansive collection of gas surrounding galaxies. Most gas and metal is actually in the CGM, quite for from the bright centers of galaxies. But because the gas does not give off much light, it is hard to study directly. The Circumgalactic Dictionary will use the open-source python packages yt and Trident to create millions of mock spectra of IllustrisTNG. This high density sample of sightlines will be used to train a machine learning algorithm to predict underlying gas properties based on observables such as column density and halo properties such as star formation rate, halo mass, etc. We had one undergraduate on this project who recently got a masters degree, and another is currently doing a first analysis of the spectral data.

Recently I joined a group that is creating a machine learning model that will use photometry to predict the chemical abundances of red giants within globyular clusters. Globular clusters are very old, dense clumps of stars that retain information about the gas they formed from, telling us about the gas that was in the Milky Way billions of years ago. The initial data gathering and processing, and succssful HST proposal, were all done by a team of undergraduates (and the PI, Jeremy Bailin). I am supervising an undergraduate in making the final model, and helping the original undergraduate team move their code and documentation to GitHub.

Current Undergraduate Research Opportunities

Current open undergraduate projects include, but are not limited to:

  • characterizing the mass and metal flow of halos that are going to stop star-formation in the near future, to predict quenching
  • finding the origins of the fuzz, and what causes the systematic differences between fuzz particles and nearby halo particles
  • contribute to initial analysis of Circumgalactic Dictionary results, focusing on connections between spectral and galactic properties
  • use our globular cluster machine learning model to create and analyze an expanded database of abundances for red giants in Milky Way globular clusters

About This Image

The main background image on this site is an edge-on TNG50 galaxy, where gas density is in purple-to-orange and the arrows represent the direction and magnitude of mass flow. These images are prepared by the TNG team and more can be found here. This particular galaxy has a mass of \(10^{11.7}M_{\odot}\), which is similar to the Milky Way, and places it in the star-forming regime. The mass is strongly outflowing along the polar regions, and there is significant inflow along the disk (mostly on the left side). My first paper stacked TNG100 halos of similar mass and feedback type to examine the average distribution of bulk material flows in the circumgalactic medium. We found that higher mass star-forming galaxies experience the most metal enrichment because their larger gravitational wells result in better retention of outflowing material. Meanwhile, more massive galaxies (\(M>10^{12.0}M_{\odot}\)) fall into the active galactic nucleus (AGN) feedback regime. This feedback quenches the galaxy and expells large amounts of material from the central region, enriching the intergalactic medium.
